K-Means & Unsupervised Learning

Discover structure in data without labels. K-Means is one of the most widely used clustering algorithms — deceptively simple but remarkably powerful when understood deeply.

🔍

What is Unsupervised Learning?

▼

From lecture: "Finding structure in data — without being told the answers."

In supervised learning, labels guide the model. In unsupervised learning, the model must discover the structure on its own — no ground truth, no labelled examples.

Supervised Learning

Input + Label → learn a mapping. Goal: predict labels for new inputs.

KNN, SVM, Decision Trees, Regression

Unsupervised Learning

Input only → discover hidden structure, groups, or representations.

K-Means, PCA, Autoencoders, DBSCAN

Why Clustering? (From Lecture)

🛍️ Customer Segmentation

Group buyers by purchase behaviour — without knowing in advance how many types exist.

🧬 Genomics

Find genes with similar expression patterns across thousands of samples.

🗞️ Document Grouping

Organize news articles into topics with no pre-defined categories.

🚨 Anomaly Detection Prep

Cluster normal behaviour first — outliers fall outside every cluster.

🗜️ Data Compression

Replace similar data points with their cluster representative (centroid). Used in vector quantization, image compression.

The K-Means Algorithm

▼

From lecture: "An iterative, assignment-update loop." K-Means alternates between two steps — assign each point to the nearest centroid, then recompute centroids as cluster means.

1
Initialise: Choose $K$ random centroids $\mu_1, \mu_2, \ldots, \mu_K$ (from the data or random).
2
Assignment Step (E-step): Assign each point $\mathbf{x}_i$ to its nearest centroid: $$c_i = \arg\min_{k} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2$$
3
Update Step (M-step): Recompute each centroid as the mean of its assigned points: $$\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|}\sum_{i \in C_k} \mathbf{x}_i$$
4
Repeat steps 2–3 until centroids stop moving (convergence) or max iterations reached.
🔑 Convergence (From Lecture)
"Convergence guaranteed — but may reach a local optimum. Run multiple times with different initialisations." The objective (inertia) decreases monotonically at each step, so it always terminates.

The Math Behind K-Means (From Lecture)

Objective Function
K-Means minimises total within-cluster variance (Inertia)
$$J = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in C_k} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2$$

$C_k$ = set of points assigned to cluster $k$ | $\mu_k$ = centroid of cluster $k$. K-Means greedily minimises $J$.

⚠️ Non-Convex Objective
From lecture: "K-Means converges to a local minimum, not necessarily the global minimum. The objective $J$ is non-convex." Different random starts can give different solutions. Always run multiple times and keep the best.
Why the mean minimises J
Optimal centroid for a fixed assignment
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu_k}\sum_{i \in C_k}\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2 = 0 \implies \mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|}\sum_{i \in C_k}\mathbf{x}_i$$

Taking derivative and setting to zero proves the centroid must be the arithmetic mean.

Distance Metrics in Clustering

▼

From lecture: "How we measure 'closeness' determines what clusters form." and "The choice of distance metric encodes your assumption about what 'similar' means."

Euclidean (L2)
$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^d (x_j - y_j)^2}$$
From Lecture
"Straight-line distance. Sensitive to scale. Default choice." Always standardise features before using Euclidean distance — otherwise features with large ranges dominate.
Manhattan (L1)
$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|_1 = \sum_{j=1}^d |x_j - y_j|$$
From Lecture
"Sum of absolute differences. More robust to outliers. Grid-like paths." When outliers are present, Manhattan distance is less influenced by a single extreme difference than Euclidean.
Cosine Similarity
$$\text{cos}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \frac{\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}}{\|\mathbf{x}\|\|\mathbf{y}\|}$$

Cosine distance = $1 - \text{cos}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$. Range: [0, 2].

From Lecture
"Measures angle, not magnitude. Used in text/NLP where direction matters." A document about 'dogs' mentioned 100 times is similar to one mentioning 'dogs' 10 times — cosine handles this, Euclidean does not.
⚠️ Metric Choice Changes Everything (From Lecture)
"Same dataset, same K=3, different distance → different clusters. Always ask: does straight-line distance make sense for my features? Are my features on the same scale? Is direction more important than magnitude?"

Inertia — Measuring Cluster Quality

▼

From lecture: "The objective function K-Means minimizes. Sum of squared distances from each point to its centroid."

Inertia
Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS)
$$\text{Inertia} = \sum_{k=1}^{K}\sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in C_k} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2$$

⬇️ Lower Inertia

Tighter, more compact clusters. Better fit. But always achievable by increasing K.

⬆️ More Clusters K

Always lowers inertia — trivially: if K=N, every point is its own centroid and inertia=0.

⚠️ Inertia Alone

Cannot tell you the right K. Need the elbow method or other criteria (BIC, silhouette).

⚠️ From Lecture
"Lower inertia = tighter, more compact clusters. More clusters K = always lower inertia (trivial limit). Inertia alone cannot tell you the right K."
📐

Choosing K — The Elbow Method

▼

From lecture: "Plot inertia vs K; pick the bend in the curve."

①
Start with K=1 and increase up to ~10
②
Record the inertia for each K after fitting
③
Plot K on x-axis, inertia on y-axis
④
Look for the "elbow" — where the marginal gain (decrease in inertia per additional cluster) flattens out
⑤
That K is your best trade-off between fit and complexity
⚠️ From Lecture
"The elbow is a heuristic, not a law. Use domain knowledge + the elbow together. Sometimes there is no clear elbow."

K-Means Limitations (From Lecture)

▼

From lecture: "Know when it fails before you deploy it."

🔢 Must Choose K

"K-Means requires you to specify the number of clusters in advance. The elbow method helps — but it's a heuristic."

🔵 Assumes Spherical Clusters

"The algorithm minimises Euclidean distance, so it expects round, equally-sized blobs. Elongated or irregular shapes fail."

📍 Sensitive to Initialisation

"Different random starting centroids can yield different final clusters. Use K-Means++ or multiple restarts."

📏 Sensitive to Scale

"Features with larger ranges dominate the distance. Always standardise (zero mean, unit variance) before clustering."

⚡ Local Optima

"K-Means converges to a local minimum, not necessarily global. The objective J is non-convex."

📊 High Dimensions Degrade

"In high-dimensional spaces, distances become meaningless (curse of dimensionality). Reduce dimensions first (PCA)."

⚡

K-Means++ — Better Initialisation

▼

K-Means++ is a smarter initialisation strategy that spreads the initial centroids far apart — dramatically reducing the chance of a bad local minimum. It is the default in sklearn.

1
Choose first centroid $\mu_1$ uniformly at random from the data points.
2
For each remaining centroid: choose the next centroid with probability proportional to $d(\mathbf{x})^2$ — the squared distance to the nearest already-chosen centroid: $$P(\mathbf{x} \text{ is chosen}) = \frac{d(\mathbf{x})^2}{\sum_{\mathbf{x}'} d(\mathbf{x}')^2}$$
3
Repeat step 2 until all $K$ centroids are selected.
4
Run standard K-Means from these better initial centroids.
🔑 Why It Works
By choosing points far from existing centroids (with higher probability), K-Means++ naturally spreads the initial centroids across the data. This leads to faster convergence and significantly better final clustering — provably no more than $O(\log K)$ times worse than optimal.

K-Means (random init)

Fast init. But can get stuck in bad local optima — especially when clusters are spread out and random starts cluster in one region.

K-Means++ ✅

Slightly slower init. But far fewer iterations needed and much better final inertia. Default in sklearn: init='k-means++'

🔬

X-Means — Automatic K Selection

▼

From lecture: X-Means extends K-Means by automatically determining the optimal number of clusters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC).

1
Start with small K (often K=2). Run K-Means.
2
Try splitting each cluster into two sub-clusters.
3
Evaluate with BIC — does the split improve the model quality? BIC penalises model complexity.
4
Accept split only if BIC improves. Reject otherwise.
5
Repeat until no more beneficial splits are found. Final K is automatically determined.
BIC
Bayesian Information Criterion — penalises model complexity
$$\text{BIC} = -2\ln(\hat{L}) + p \cdot \ln(n)$$

$\hat{L}$ = model likelihood | $p$ = number of parameters | $n$ = number of data points. Lower BIC = better model.

Week 6 Quiz

▼

🧩 Test Your Understanding

Q1. In the K-Means update step, the new centroid for cluster $k$ is set to:

The centroid update sets $\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|}\sum_{i \in C_k}\mathbf{x}_i$ — the mean. This can be derived by minimising the within-cluster sum of squares.

Q2. Why does increasing K always decrease inertia?

At K=N (each point is its own centroid), inertia = 0. More centroids always allow closer assignments. This is why inertia alone can't determine the right K.

Q3. Which distance metric is best for text/NLP clustering, where direction matters more than magnitude?

From the lecture: "Cosine measures angle, not magnitude. Used in text/NLP where direction matters." A long document and a short document about the same topic have the same direction, so cosine similarity is high.

Q4. K-Means++ improves on standard K-Means by:

K-Means++ chooses each new centroid with probability $\propto d(\mathbf{x})^2$ — favouring points far from existing centroids. This spreads centroids naturally, avoiding poor local optima.

Q5. X-Means uses ___ to decide whether to split a cluster:

From the lecture: "Evaluate whether the split improves the model using a statistical criterion like Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Accept the split if BIC improves."

Q6. K-Means assumes clusters are:

From lecture: "The algorithm minimises Euclidean distance, so it expects round, equally-sized blobs. Elongated or irregular shapes fail." Use DBSCAN or GMM for non-spherical clusters.

Numerical Problems

▼

Problem 1 — One Full K-Means Iteration

Data points: $A=(1,1)$, $B=(2,1)$, $C=(4,3)$, $D=(5,4)$, $E=(1,2)$.
Initial centroids: $\mu_1 = (1,1)$, $\mu_2 = (5,4)$. Use $K=2$ and Euclidean distance.
Perform one full iteration (assignment + update).
Distances to μ₁=(1,1)
$d(A,\mu_1)=\sqrt{0+0}=0$, $d(B,\mu_1)=\sqrt{1+0}=1$
$d(C,\mu_1)=\sqrt{9+4}=\sqrt{13}\approx3.61$
$d(D,\mu_1)=\sqrt{16+9}=5$, $d(E,\mu_1)=\sqrt{0+1}=1$
Distances to μ₂=(5,4)
$d(A,\mu_2)=\sqrt{16+9}=5$, $d(B,\mu_2)=\sqrt{9+9}=\sqrt{18}\approx4.24$
$d(C,\mu_2)=\sqrt{1+1}=\sqrt{2}\approx1.41$
$d(D,\mu_2)=0$, $d(E,\mu_2)=\sqrt{16+4}=\sqrt{20}\approx4.47$
Assignment
$A$: 0 < 5 → Cluster 1   $B$: 1 < 4.24 → Cluster 1
$C$: 3.61 > 1.41 → Cluster 2   $D$: 5 > 0 → Cluster 2
$E$: 1 < 4.47 → Cluster 1
Cluster 1: {A, B, E}   Cluster 2: {C, D}
Update Centroids
$\mu_1 = \frac{(1,1)+(2,1)+(1,2)}{3} = \left(\frac{4}{3}, \frac{4}{3}\right) \approx (1.33, 1.33)$
$\mu_2 = \frac{(4,3)+(5,4)}{2} = (4.5, 3.5)$

Problem 2 — Compute Inertia

After clustering, Cluster 1 has points $\{(1,1),(2,1),(1,2)\}$ with centroid $(1.33, 1.33)$.
Cluster 2 has points $\{(4,3),(5,4)\}$ with centroid $(4.5, 3.5)$.
Compute the total inertia.
Cluster 1
$(1-1.33)^2+(1-1.33)^2 = 0.109+0.109 = 0.218$
$(2-1.33)^2+(1-1.33)^2 = 0.449+0.109 = 0.558$
$(1-1.33)^2+(2-1.33)^2 = 0.109+0.449 = 0.558$
Cluster 1 WCSS $= 0.218+0.558+0.558 = \mathbf{1.334}$
Cluster 2
$(4-4.5)^2+(3-3.5)^2 = 0.25+0.25 = 0.5$
$(5-4.5)^2+(4-3.5)^2 = 0.25+0.25 = 0.5$
Cluster 2 WCSS $= 0.5+0.5 = \mathbf{1.0}$
Total Inertia
$\text{Inertia} = 1.334 + 1.0 = \mathbf{2.334}$ ✅

Problem 3 — K-Means++ First Centroid Probability

First centroid $\mu_1 = (0,0)$ already chosen. Three candidate points for $\mu_2$:
$P_1=(1,0)$, $P_2=(3,0)$, $P_3=(6,0)$.
Compute the K-Means++ probability of selecting each point as the second centroid.
Squared distances to μ₁
$d(P_1, \mu_1)^2 = (1-0)^2+(0-0)^2 = 1$
$d(P_2, \mu_1)^2 = (3-0)^2 = 9$
$d(P_3, \mu_1)^2 = (6-0)^2 = 36$
Total $= 1+9+36 = 46$
Probabilities
$P(\text{choose }P_1) = \frac{1}{46} \approx 2.2\%$
$P(\text{choose }P_2) = \frac{9}{46} \approx 19.6\%$
$P(\text{choose }P_3) = \frac{36}{46} \approx \mathbf{78.3\%}$
Interpret
K-Means++ would most likely choose $P_3=(6,0)$ — the point farthest from the first centroid. This ensures the two centroids are well-spread. ✅

Problem 4 — Cosine Similarity

Compute the cosine similarity between two document vectors:
$\mathbf{x} = [2, 1, 0, 3]$ and $\mathbf{y} = [1, 0, 1, 2]$.
Then compute cosine distance.
Dot product
$\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{y} = 2(1)+1(0)+0(1)+3(2) = 2+0+0+6 = 8$
Magnitudes
$\|\mathbf{x}\| = \sqrt{4+1+0+9} = \sqrt{14} \approx 3.742$
$\|\mathbf{y}\| = \sqrt{1+0+1+4} = \sqrt{6} \approx 2.449$
Cosine Similarity
$\text{cos}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \frac{8}{3.742 \times 2.449} = \frac{8}{9.165} \approx \mathbf{0.873}$
Cosine Distance $= 1 - 0.873 = \mathbf{0.127}$ — very similar documents ✅
